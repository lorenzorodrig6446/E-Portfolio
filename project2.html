Background

Reimagining Delivery
Through Autonomy

Last-mile package delivery is one of the most costly and congested segments of modern logistics. Drone delivery has emerged as a compelling solution — but the prevailing model assumes a fixed landing pad. What if the drone could land on a moving vehicle instead, dramatically expanding delivery range and eliminating the need for dedicated infrastructure?

This project, inspired by the MBZIRC 2017 Robotics Competition, challenges exactly that assumption. The task: design an autonomous guidance, navigation, and control (GNC) system capable of flying a quadrotor UAV from a stationary origin and landing it precisely on a van moving in a circular path — with no human input during flight.

Beyond the academic challenge, this scenario represents a genuine frontier in autonomous systems. Precise dynamic landing on a moving platform — accounting for relative motion, vehicle attitude, and smooth descent — is a capability with direct applications in delivery logistics, naval operations, and emergency response. Mastering it in simulation is the essential first step toward real-world deployment.

Control Architecture

🎯
Guidance — Outer Loop
PID position controller with velocity and acceleration feedforward terms. Tracks the moving van's circular trajectory in 3D space and generates acceleration commands.
↓
🔄
Attitude Control — Inner Loop
PID attitude controller regulating roll, pitch, and yaw. Receives commanded attitude derived from guidance accelerations and computes required control moments.
↓
⚙️
Control Allocation — Mixer
Maps thrust and moment commands to four individual motor throttle signals (0–1) using a mixer matrix, respecting motor saturation limits.
↓
🚁
Vehicle Dynamics Model
16-state nonlinear quadrotor model including motor lag (τ = 28 ms), rigid body dynamics, and NED-frame equations of motion. Simulated in MATLAB at 100 Hz.

My Role

GNC Engineer

This was a fully individual project — every line of MATLAB code, every design decision, and every tuning iteration was my own work. Starting from a bare simulation framework, I designed and implemented the complete guidance, navigation, and control stack from scratch.

📐

Attitude Controller Design

Designed and tuned a full PID attitude controller governing roll, pitch, and yaw. Validated stability and response quality through step-input simulations, achieving fast convergence with low overshoot across all three axes.

🗺️

Guidance System Design

Developed a cascaded outer-loop PID guidance system with velocity feedforward to track the moving platform's circular trajectory. Implemented velocity saturation limiting (3 m/s) and a multi-phase altitude profile for safe approach and landing.

💻

Simulation & Tuning

Implemented the full GNC stack in MATLAB, iteratively tuning 18 PID gains across position and attitude controllers. Determined that a 150-second simulation window minimized terminal error, achieving a final competition score of 11.80.

Simulation Results

60-Second Trajectory

Early simulation run (60 s) showing the drone tracking and approaching the van's circular path before landing. The drone climbs, intercepts the trajectory, and descends onto the platform.

Motor Command Convergence

All four motor throttle signals converge tightly to ~0.48 within seconds of takeoff and hold steady throughout the cruise phase, confirming a well-tuned, stable controller. The brief divergence at t ≈ 148 s corresponds to the final landing maneuver.

Summary

Control That Converges

This project required building something that actually works — not just a theoretical design, but a fully implemented, tuned, and validated GNC system that could land a simulated drone on a target moving at unknown speed and radius. Every design choice had to be justified through simulation data.

🎯

Final Competition Score: 11.80
Scored against class peers using position, velocity, attitude & heading accuracy at touchdown. Lower is better — ideal score is zero.

  • Cascaded control is powerful: Separating attitude and position control into inner and outer loops allowed each to be independently designed and validated, making the full system far more tractable to tune and debug.
  • Feedforward matters for moving targets: Pure feedback control introduces lag when tracking a continuously moving platform. Incorporating velocity feedforward terms dramatically improved position tracking accuracy during the approach phase.
  • Simulation time is a design variable: Empirical testing revealed that allowing 150 seconds — rather than the default — gave the guidance system sufficient time to smoothly converge on the target, reducing landing error significantly.
  • Autonomy has real-world stakes: The scenario — landing on a delivery van — is not hypothetical. Companies like Wing and Zipline are actively solving versions of this problem. Building intuition for it in simulation translates directly to the industry.
  • Individual work builds deeper understanding: Having to design, implement, debug, and tune every component alone forced a level of system-level understanding that collaborative projects rarely require.
