Background

Fighting Wildfires
With Autonomy

Wildfire management in the United States has fundamentally changed since the late 1980s. Once viewed purely as destructive, fires are now recognized as ecologically necessary — but their threat to communities, infrastructure, and human life remains. As of 2025, resources available to manage active fires are chronically limited, and every deployment of human personnel carries serious risk.

This project was issued by the National Park Service (NPS) as a formal Request for Proposals: design, build, and fly a small autonomous UAV capable of detecting and suppressing a simulated wildfire — entirely without human intervention during flight. Beyond the academic challenge, this project demonstrates how aerospace autonomy extends far beyond the defense sector, offering life-saving capabilities in civilian emergency response.

For me personally, this capstone was a formative experience — bridging coursework in structures, CAD, and autonomy into a single, high-stakes system design that had to actually fly and compete.

Mission Requirements

Configuration Quadcopter (autonomous)
Max TOGW 1.0 kg
Budget ≤ $350 per team
GPS Not permitted — indoor positioning only
Fire Detection Provided thermal sensor (MLX90640)
Hover Requirement ≥ 5 s over fire area
Descent Target Within 0.5 m of fire
Payload ≤ 25 g solid fire suppressant
Flight Computer Raspberry Pi 4 + Navio2
Competition 5 scored flight attempts, April 2026

My Role

Structural Engineer

As the team's structural engineer, I was responsible for the physical integrity and manufacturability of the vehicle — from the first CAD sketch to the physical test stand used for gain tuning. My work sat at the intersection of design, analysis, and fabrication.

🖥️

CAD Design

Developed the full 3D CAD assembly of the quadrotor in SolidWorks, integrating the frame, avionics stack (Raspberry Pi 4 + Navio2), propulsion system, and payload mechanism into a cohesive design within the 1 kg TOGW constraint.

📐

FEA — Structural Analysis

Performed Finite Element Analysis on the drone arms to verify structural integrity under the lift forces generated during flight. Analysis confirmed stresses remained well below the material's yield strength, validating the arm geometry and cross-section.

🔧

Test Stand Design & Build

Designed and fabricated the physical test stand used to safely mount and constrain the drone during initial motor spin-up, gain tuning, and control system verification — a required milestone per the RFP before any free flight attempt.

Design Work

FEA — Arm Structural Analysis

Von Mises stress distribution on the drone arm under the motor lift force during flight. Peak stresses remain well below the yield strength of 1.39 × 10⁸ N/m², confirming structural margin.

Test Stand — CAD Design

CAD model of the test stand designed to constrain the vehicle during motor spin-up and control system tuning — a required pre-flight milestone in the RFP.

Summary

Engineering That Flies

This project demanded real systems-level thinking — every structural decision had downstream consequences for weight, balance, and flight performance. Working within a strict $350 budget and a 1 kg weight ceiling pushed creative problem-solving at every step.

🏆

1st Place — Class Competition
Recognized for excellence in Kalman filter design, GNC, structures, electronics, and lean design.

  • Structural integrity under real loads: FEA on the drone arms validated the design could sustain motor lift forces with sufficient factor of safety — analysis that directly informed geometry decisions.
  • CAD as a design tool, not just documentation: The SolidWorks assembly drove integration decisions, revealing component conflicts and layout trade-offs before any physical parts were ordered.
  • Test infrastructure is part of the design: Building the test stand wasn't a supporting task — it was a core engineering deliverable that enabled safe, systematic control tuning.
  • Constraints sharpen engineering judgment: Weight, cost, and indoor flight restrictions forced disciplined trade-off thinking across every subsystem — a direct preview of production-level aerospace design.
  • Autonomy beyond defense: The mission context — civilian wildfire response — highlighted how autonomous systems can serve critical humanitarian roles, expanding what I see as the scope of aerospace engineering.
